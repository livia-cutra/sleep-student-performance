Student Lifestyle, Study Habits, and Academic Performance

Exploring how sleep, study time, motivation, and other lifestyle factors relate to exam success

Vitrinnie Asante-Tannor • Isabella Boateng • Livia Cutra • David Gyamfi

Introduction

This project explores how sleep, study habits, and other lifestyle and background factors influence student academic performance. By comparing behavioral and environmental variables, we aim to better understand which patterns are most strongly associated with exam success.

Dataset Overview

  • 6,608 students
  • 13 features
  • Source: Kaggle Student Performance Dataset

Our Goal

Identify which academic, behavioral, and lifestyle factors most strongly affect student exam performance and present those relationships through clear and interactive visualizations.

Key Visualizations

Distribution of Exam Scores

This visualization shows that the exam scores for the 6,607 students follow a normal distribution, centered around a score of approximately 68. The majority of students achieve scores within a relatively narrow range of 60 to 75, with very few individuals scoring below 50 or above 85. This indicates a highly consistent performance across the student body with most learners achieving moderate success.

Sleep Duration vs. Exam Score

This visualization explores how sleep duration relates to exam performance. It helps identify whether moderate sleep ranges are associated with stronger academic outcomes compared to very low or very high sleep durations.

Hours Studied vs. Exam Score

This scatterplot shows the relationship between hours studied and exam score, with points colored by school type and interactive filters for exploration. Overall, students who study more hours tend to achieve higher exam scores, although the variation within each group suggests that study time alone does not fully determine performance.

Exam Scores by Sleep Category

This box plot compares exam score distributions across sleep categories. It highlights differences in central tendency and spread, making it easier to see whether certain sleep ranges are associated with stronger or weaker academic outcomes.

Impact of Motivation and Resources on Exam Scores

This line chart compares average exam scores across levels of access to resources and motivation. It helps show how non-academic factors interact and whether increased support and motivation are associated with improved student performance.

Study Hours, Sleep, and Exam Performance

This heatmap shows how combinations of study hours and sleep hours relate to student performance. Cells with darker color indicate either higher average exam scores or greater student concentration, depending on the selected setting. The visualization suggests that academic performance is shaped by the balance between studying and sleep rather than by one factor alone.

Motivation vs. Performance

This visualization highlights a clear positive relationship between a student's self-reported motivation level and their actual exam performance. Students in the "High" motivation category have a distribution that is noticeably shifted toward higher scores compared to those in the "Low" motivation group. While all three groups maintain a bell-shaped distribution, the peak (mode) for highly motivated students is significantly further to the right on the x-axis, suggesting that higher motivation is a key driver for higher academic achievement.

Study Time vs Sleep Balance

This visualization compares study time and sleep together to examine how balanced habits relate to performance. It highlights that strong outcomes are often associated with combinations of adequate sleep and consistent study time rather than extremes of either behavior.

Key Takeaways

Academic success is multi-dimensional

Exam performance is not explained by sleep or study time alone. The visualizations suggest that student outcomes are shaped by a combination of behavioral, academic, and background factors.

Study habits matter most in context

More study time is generally associated with stronger performance, but its impact becomes more meaningful when viewed alongside sleep patterns, attendance, and other lifestyle behaviors.

Resources and motivation influence outcomes

Differences in motivation and access-related variables suggest that academic performance is also connected to the broader environment students learn in, not just the effort they put in individually.

Conclusion

Student academic performance is shaped by more than hours spent studying. Across our visualizations, three patterns stand out. First, sleep and study time work together; students with balanced habits consistently outperform those who prioritize one at the expense of the other. Second, motivation and access to resources amplify academic effort; students with higher motivation and better resources tend to score higher even when study time is similar. Third, no single factor determines outcomes; the strongest predictor of exam performance appears to be the combination of consistent behavioral habits rather than any individual variable. Future work could explore whether interventions targeting sleep consistency or resource access would have a measurable impact on scores, and whether these patterns hold across different school types or income levels.
