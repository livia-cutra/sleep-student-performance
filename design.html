Design Decisions

This page explains how we designed our final project site to present student performance patterns clearly, consistently, and interactively. Our choices were guided by ideas from class, including visual hierarchy, chart selection, narrative flow, and audience-centered design.

Our Design Goals

We wanted the site to feel like a connected visual story rather than a collection of separate charts. Our main goals were clarity, consistency, and helping viewers move easily from broad patterns to more detailed comparisons.

Clarity

We used figure labels, readable spacing, and “How to Read This Chart” cards to make each visualization easier to understand quickly.

Consistency

Repeated card layouts, purple accent colors, and consistent labels helped the project feel visually unified even as chart types changed.

Usability

We kept interactions that improved interpretation and removed controls that felt unnecessary or distracting after feedback.

Visual and Layout Decisions

The page structure was designed to guide viewers through the project smoothly. We used an intro screen, a simple navbar, and a card-based layout so the site would feel polished and easy to navigate.

Intro Screen

We added an opening screen to create a stronger first impression and make the project feel more presentation-ready.

Navigation Bar

The navbar improves flow by letting viewers jump directly to the visualizations, takeaways, and design reflection page.

Figure Labels

Numbering each chart made the visuals easier to reference during presentation and discussion.

How-to-Read Cards

These cards help viewers understand what to focus on before reading each chart more deeply.

Why We Chose These Visualizations

Each visualization was selected to answer a specific question about student performance. We focused on matching chart type to task—distribution, comparison, or relationship—while keeping designs simple and readable.

Histogram (Distribution)

A histogram with a small bin size was used to clearly show the overall score distribution. A single color keeps the focus on shape, while tooltips provide exact counts for each range.

Faceted Chart (Motivation)

A faceted layout allows direct comparison across motivation levels while keeping scales consistent. Distinct colors reinforce the progression from low to high motivation.

Line Chart (Trends)

The line chart highlights how scores change across resource levels for different motivation groups. It was chosen to show trends more clearly than individual variation.

Box Plot (Spread)

A box plot summarizes score distributions across study categories using medians and spread. Overlaying points adds detail without losing the overall structure.

Bar Chart (Comparison)

A grouped bar chart makes it easy to compare public vs. private schools across categories. Side-by-side placement improves readability and direct comparison.

Heatmap (Two Variables)

The heatmap shows how study and sleep interact in a compact grid. A fixed color scale keeps comparisons accurate across filters.

Scatterplot (Relationship)

The scatterplot preserves individual points while showing trends. Separate regression lines make school-type differences easier to see.

Treemap (Distribution by Group)

The treemap replaced the Sankey to improve readability. It makes smaller groups easier to compare and better supports distribution analysis.

What We Applied from Class

This project reflects several ideas we practiced throughout the semester, especially narrative structure, chart-task fit, audience-centered design, and iterative revision.

Visual Storytelling

We arranged the figures from overview to comparison to deeper relationship analysis so the site would read as a coherent story.

Chart-Task Fit

Different chart types were chosen based on the kind of question we wanted to answer: distribution, comparison, trend, or relationship.

Audience-Centered Design

We designed the site for viewers who may not already know the dataset, which is why we added labels, guidance cards, and consistent section structure.

Iteration

Feedback helped us revise chart order, replace less readable visuals, refine interactions, and improve layout consistency across the final site.

How Feedback Improved the Final Site

Our final site became stronger through revision. We reordered figures to improve flow, revised the grouped bar chart for direct public/private comparison, replaced the Sankey with a clearer treemap, separated regression lines in the scatterplot, fixed the heatmap legend scale, and added figure labels plus explanation cards to improve readability.
